Brief Summary

This paper reveals a fundamental vulnerability in modern online HD map construction models such as MapTR and VectorMapNet. These models exhibit a strong symmetry bias, often mispredicting asymmetric roads—like forks, merges, or sharp turns—as symmetric or straight. This flaw arises from training data imbalance and architectural design, and it can lead to unsafe planning behaviors such as missed turns or unreachable routes. To exploit this weakness, the authors introduce the first physical-world attacks on online map construction using simple roadside tools: a flashlight for camera blinding and adversarial patches for white-box attacks. Their two-stage framework first detects vulnerable asymmetric scenes and then optimizes roadside attack locations. Experiments on the nuScenes dataset and real-world tests show that these attacks significantly degrade map accuracy and increase unsafe planned trajectories. As a partial defense, the paper proposes fine-tuning with asymmetric data to improve robustness. A key limitation is that the attack is most effective only when the vehicle is near the interference source, and its impact weakens quickly once the vehicle passes it. Despite this, the work demonstrates a critical and previously overlooked safety risk in autonomous driving systems.

Slide 03: Motivation



This slide explains why online HD maps are becoming widely used and why their security is important. Since traditional HD maps are expensive to maintain, vehicles rely on online mapping, which is still vulnerable to physical interference. The example shows how a simple roadside flashlight or adversarial patch can mislead the map model, motivating the need for this research.














Slide 04: Background (AD Systems)



This slide explains the key components of an autonomous driving (AD) system. AD relies on perception, trajectory prediction, and motion planning, all of which depend on accurate environmental understanding. Mapping is a crucial part of perception because it provides detailed geometric and semantic information about the road. Since motion planning uses this map information to generate safe driving paths, the reliability and robustness of HD maps are essential for ensuring the overall safety of autonomous vehicles.

Slide 05: Background (Online Map Construction)



This slide compares traditional HD map construction with online map construction. Traditional HD maps require heavy manual data collection and annotation, making them expensive and quickly outdated. Online map construction addresses this by predicting map elements directly from real-time sensor data, enabling dynamic and continuously updated HD maps. Modern approaches such as VectorMapNet and MapTR use query-based decoding to improve both accuracy and efficiency, making them widely adopted in current autonomous driving systems.
















Slide 06: Vulnerability Analysis (Dataset + Real World)



This slide shows that online HD map models often misinterpret asymmetric roads as symmetric. Both dataset tests and real-world vehicle experiments confirm this bias, and optimized flashlight interference makes the problem worse. This means a natural model weakness can become a safety risk when triggered physically.

Slide 07: Vulnerability Analysis (Model Behavior)



This slide explains that the model architecture itself contributes to the vulnerability. Because the BEV encoder and map decoder rely heavily on nearby features, the model is easily influenced and tends to force asymmetric roads into symmetric shapes. This leads to errors like road straightening or early-turn predictions.


















Slide 08: Threat Model



This slide explains the goal and ability of the attacker. The attacker aims to exploit the model’s symmetry bias to make the online map incorrectly predict road structure. Two attack objectives are introduced: Road Straightening and Early Turn. The attacker operates offline, has no access to the vehicle, and can only place interference on the roadside using limited tools such as a flashlight or small adversarial patch.

Slide 09: Attack Design



This slide summarizes the two-stage attack framework. First, the system automatically identifies asymmetric scenes that are vulnerable to manipulation. Next, it searches for the best roadside positions for physical interference (flashlight or adversarial patch). The design addresses three challenges: detecting attack-prone scenes, navigating a large search space, and producing effective real-world attacks on online map construction models.






















Slide 10: Attack Design Overview



This slide summarizes the two-stage attack process.
In Stage 1, the system automatically detects asymmetric scenes using a rule-based classifier and then refines the results with a vision-language model to remove false positives.
In Stage 2, the framework ranks roadside attack positions and simulates how a flashlight or adversarial patch would affect the camera view, choosing the most effective configuration to trigger the mapping error.

Slide 11: Attack Design: Identify Asymmetric Scenario



This slide explains why identifying asymmetric scenes is important for the attack. Since datasets do not label which scenes are symmetric or where turns begin, the system must detect them automatically. It does this by locating asymmetric road patterns and pinpointing “asymmetry anchors,” which are the best points for placing physical interference during the attack.
















Slide 12: Attack Design – Two-Step Asymmetric Scene Classifier



This slide explains how the system detects asymmetric scenes.
In Step 1, a rule-based geometric classifier looks for large curvature differences between the left and right road boundaries and marks the divergence points as asymmetry anchors.
In Step 2, a vision-language model (GPT-4o) refines these results by checking map data and images to confirm whether the scene is truly asymmetric. This ensures only accurate asymmetric cases are passed to the next stage of the attack.

Slide 13: Attack Design – VLM Prompt and Example



This slide shows how the vision-language model improves scene classification. The VLM uses maps, boundary coordinates, and camera images to understand road geometry and correct mistakes made by the rule-based classifier. The example demonstrates the VLM successfully identifying symmetry or asymmetry with detailed reasoning, leading to more reliable attack planning.
















Slide 14: Attack Design – Attack Position Ranking (Concept)



This slide explains why the attack needs a ranking system for roadside positions.
Searching an entire 3D space is too large and unrealistic, so the authors use a lightweight scoring method. The attack is strongest when the interference is close to the cameras and near the asymmetry anchors, so these factors guide which positions are ranked highest for launching the attack.

Slide 15: Attack Design – Scoring Function Details



This slide shows how the ranking score is computed.
Each candidate position is evaluated based on two factors: how intense the interference would be at the camera (distance) and how well it aligns with the asymmetry anchor (angle). By combining these factors into a single score, the system selects a small set of top-k positions for final attack optimization.














Slide 16: Attack Objectives (Road Straightening & Early Turn)



This slide explains the two goals of the attack.
In Road Straightening, the attacker tries to make the model convert a curved or diverging boundary into a straight one. In Early Turn, the attacker pushes the predicted boundary outward so the model believes a turn starts earlier than it really does. Both objectives use boundary-based losses that guide the model toward these incorrect road shapes when interference is applied.

Slide 17: Attack Configuration Optimization (Four Configurations)



This slide shows how the attack is applied using two methods camera blinding (black-box) and adversarial patches (white-box) combined with the two attack objectives (straightening and early turn). Camera blinding uses a search strategy to find the best roadside position, while the patch attack optimizes both the position and the patch pattern. The final attack is chosen based on which configuration produces the lowest loss for the targeted road manipulation.


















Slide 18: Experiments Overview



This slide summarizes the two types of experiments conducted.
The dataset experiments use 100 asymmetric scenes from nuScenes to test multiple map-construction models under flashlight and patch attacks.
The real-world experiments use a custom vehicle with six cameras and evaluate attacks at real intersections and road splits under different weather and lighting conditions.
Together, these experiments show both simulated and real-world impacts of the proposed attacks.

Slide 19: Dataset Results – Road Straightening Attack



This slide presents the effect of the road-straightening attack on mapping accuracy and route planning.
Both flashlight and adversarial patch attacks reduce boundary AP by nearly 10%, showing a clear drop in map quality.
More importantly, unreachable routes jump from 27% to 44%, meaning almost half of the planned paths fail so the vehicle cannot find a safe way to the destination.












Slide 20: Dataset Results – Early Turn Attack



This slide presents the impact of the early-turn attack.
The attack slightly lowers mapping accuracy (Boundary AP drops by ~3–5%) but has a much bigger effect on planning. Unreachable routes rise from 10% to 27%, meaning the model becomes confused about where the turn begins. This can cause up to 27% of planned paths to collide with real road boundaries, highlighting a serious safety threat.

Slide 21: Attack Effectiveness – Road Straightening Attack



This slide compares the proposed attack to baseline methods.
For road straightening, the authors’ attack achieves the lowest map AP, showing it is the most effective at degrading map quality. It also causes the highest unreachable goal rate (44%), outperforming random sampling and PSO. These results confirm that the proposed method is both stronger and more targeted than baseline attack strategies.














Slide 22: Attack Effectiveness – Early Turn Attacks



This slide shows the performance of the early-turn attack.
Although the accuracy drop is smaller than in the straightening attack, the early-turn attack significantly increases unsafe driving behavior. The unsafe trajectory rate rises from 10% to up to 27%, meaning the vehicle is more likely to drift toward road boundaries. This confirms that early-turn attacks can directly impact driving safety even with modest changes in map accuracy.

Slide 23: Real-World Attacks Results



This slide summarizes the real-world attack performance using a test vehicle equipped with multiple cameras.
Road straightening attacks are highly effective: flashlight interference succeeds in all 8 scenes, while adversarial patches work in most cases. Early-turn attacks are harder but still successful in several scenarios. These results demonstrate that simple roadside tools like a flashlight or printed patch can reliably mislead real autonomous driving systems.






















Slide 24: Attack Works in Different Conditions



This slide shows that the attacks remain effective under various real-world conditions.
They work in cloudy and nighttime environments, and can still succeed even when pedestrians or passing vehicles partially block the camera. This demonstrates that the attacks are not limited to controlled laboratory settings they hold up in realistic, everyday driving scenarios.

Slide 25: Attack Other State-of-the-art Models



This slide evaluates the attack on additional advanced HD map models, including those using LiDAR–camera fusion.
Across models like MapTR, MapQR, and GeMap, the attacks consistently reduce mapping accuracy and significantly increase unreachable or unsafe planned trajectories. This shows that the vulnerability is widespread and affects multiple state-of-the-art approaches, not just a single model.
















Slide 26: Limitations



This slide explains the main weaknesses of the attack.
The attack only works when the vehicle is close to the flashlight or patch, and the effect quickly fades after passing it. It is less effective on LiDAR–camera fusion systems, though it still causes some errors. Environmental factors also matter patches are hard to see at night, while flashlights are more noticeable and easier for humans to detect.

Slide 27: Relation to CPS



This slide connects the work to Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS).
The attacks reveal real-world adversarial vulnerabilities in camera-based perception, which can lead to unsafe driving decisions. Although sensor fusion adds robustness, data bias and rare scene types still cause failures. Because planning depends directly on mapping, a manipulated map can trigger dangerous actions, showing how tightly coupled perception and control are in CPS.


Slide 28: Related Work & Follow-Up Research (Part 1)





This slide highlights foundational papers in online HD map construction.
HDMapNet introduced the first framework for building HD maps directly from onboard sensors. VectorMapNet advanced this by predicting vectorized map elements end-to-end. MapTR then improved accuracy and structure using a transformer-based design. These works laid the groundwork for modern online HD mapping systems that the current paper studies and attacks.

Slide 29: Related Work & Follow-Up Research (Part 2)




This slide presents more recent developments in HD map learning and robustness.
InstaGraM models HD maps as instance-level graphs for better flexibility and long-range accuracy. ADMap focuses on stabilizing jagged map predictions to improve planning safety. The WIP/USENIX work investigates HD map tampering threats, showing growing interest in map security. Together, these papers complement the current study by addressing scalability, stability, and security of HD map systems.


























Slide 30: Future Research Directions




This slide outlines potential next steps for both defending against and extending the attacks.
On the defense side, improvements include adding more asymmetric scenes to training data, redesigning model architectures to reduce unwanted boundary interactions, and verifying predictions using multiple sensor types. On the attack side, future work could explore moving or continuous attacks, combining camera and LiDAR attacks, using invisible infrared light, or scaling attacks across larger city areas.

Summary of Ideas Shared by Groups for discussion

Across the class discussion, most groups agreed that autonomous vehicles should undergo formal adversarial robustness testing before being allowed on public roads, since physical-world attacks like glare, spoofed patterns, or manipulated boundaries can cause real safety hazards. Groups highlighted that current systems rely too heavily on cameras, making them vulnerable, and suggested strengthening defenses by combining multiple sensing modalities such as LiDAR, radar, and cameras to cross-validate predictions. Another common idea was enriching training datasets with more asymmetric and rare road structures so models learn to handle unusual scenes more reliably and also emphasized implementing real-time anomaly detection for example, flagging abnormal brightness spikes, inconsistent boundaries, or sudden shape deviations and comparing outputs across sensors to quickly identify when the map construction model is being misled. Overall, the shared view was that improving redundancy, diversity of training data, and early-warning detection mechanisms are crucial for safer autonomous navigation.
